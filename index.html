BACKGROUND - ~1.5 min

So this is the standard 2PC setup from Yao '86. Two parties, $P_1$ has input $x$ from $X_n$, $P_2$ has input $y$ from $Y_n$. They want to compute $f$ together, which gives outputs in $Z^1_n \times Z^2_n$. $n$ is the security parameter.

Security is defined by the real-ideal paradigm. Two columns here.

Point to the left column.

Left side is the real world. No trusted party, they just run some protocol $\Pi$. The adversary controls one party and can do whatever it wants. Output is $\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)$.

Point to the right column.

Right side is the ideal world. There's a trusted party that takes inputs, computes $f(x,y)$, gives outputs. The simulator $\mathcal{S}$ can only talk to this trusted party. Output is $\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)$.

A protocol is secure if for every adversary, there's a simulator that makes these two distributions look the same.

Point to the table.

We want four properties. Privacy, correctness, input independence, all doable since Yao. But fairness, where both get output or neither does, that's impossible. Cleve showed this in '86.

Secure Two-Party Computation

Two parties $P_1$ (input $x \in X_n$) and $P_2$ (input $y \in Y_n$) jointly compute $f_n : X_n \times Y_n \to Z^1_n \times Z^2_n$, where $n$ is the security parameter. Security is defined via the real/ideal paradigm.

Real world

No trusted party. Parties execute protocol $\Pi$. Adversary $\mathcal{A}$ controls one party, sends arbitrary messages, can abort at any time.

Output: $\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)$ = joint output of $\mathcal{A}$ and the honest party.

Ideal world

A trusted party receives inputs, computes $f(x,y)$, delivers outputs. Adversary $\mathcal{S}$ (simulator) can only interact with the trusted party.

Output: $\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)$ = joint output of $\mathcal{S}$ and the honest party.

Security properties

  • Privacy: adversary's view can be simulated from output alone (achievable, Yao 86)
  • Correctness: honest party always gets $f^i(x,y)$ (achievable)
  • Input independence: adversary commits input before seeing honest party's (achievable)
  • Fairness: both get output, or neither does (impossible, Cleve 86)

CLEVE'S IMPOSSIBILITY - ~1.5 min

This is Cleve's 1986 result. It's the starting point for the whole paper.

Cleve showed: any $r$-round coin-flipping protocol, a cheater can bias the outcome by $\Omega(1/r)$. So complete fairness is impossible for general 2PC.

The idea is simple. Let $a_i$ be what $P_1$ outputs if $P_2$ aborts after round $i$, and $b_i$ for the other way. At round 0, nobody knows anything. At round $r$, both know the answer. So somewhere in between, one party learns it first. That party can just quit and walk away with the answer while the other gets nothing. The advantage is $\Omega(1/r)$. This is tight, Moran, Naor, Segev showed $O(1/r)$ works for coin tossing in 2009.

Point to the bottom grid.

So after Cleve, people settled for security-with-abort. The trusted party computes $f(x,y)$, gives $z_1$ to $P_1$ first, then $P_1$ says continue or abort. If abort, $P_2$ gets nothing.

This keeps privacy, correctness, input independence. But fairness is zero. $P_1$ can always learn the answer and block $P_2$. Goldreich flagged in his textbook, Section 7.7.1.1, that defining partial fairness was an open problem. That's what this paper solves.

Cleve's Impossibility (1986)

Theorem [Cleve 86]: For any $r$-round two-party coin-flipping protocol, a malicious adversary can bias the outcome by $\Omega(1/r)$. Complete fairness is impossible for general 2PC without honest majority.

The argument (intuition)

  • Let $a_i$ = $P_1$'s output if $P_2$ aborts after round $i$;   $b_i$ = $P_2$'s output if $P_1$ aborts after round $i$
  • At round 0: $a_0, b_0$ carry no information about $f(x,y)$ (no messages exchanged yet)
  • At round $r$: $a_r = b_r = f(x,y)$ (both know the answer)
  • So both $a_i$ and $b_i$ must transition from "uninformed" to "correct" as $i$ goes from $0$ to $r$
  • They can't both transition at the same round, so one party must learn first
  • At that threshold round, that party aborts: it has $f(x,y)$, the other has garbage
  • Advantage: $\Omega(1/r)$ (tight: Moran-Naor-Segev 2009 showed $O(1/r)$ is achievable for coin tossing)

Consequence: Security-with-abort

How it works

  • Trusted party computes $f(x,y) = (z_1, z_2)$
  • Sends $z_1$ to $P_1$ first
  • $P_1$ then sends continue or abort
  • If abort: trusted party sends $\bot$ to $P_2$

Properties

  • Privacy: preserved (negligible gap)
  • Correctness: preserved
  • Input independence: preserved
  • Fairness: none

Goldreich (Section 7.7.1.1): defining partial fairness was an open problem.

THE DEFINITION - ~2 min

This is the main definition, Definition 1 from Section 2.

Read the definition box.

A protocol $1/p$-securely computes $f$ if for every adversary, there's a simulator in the fair ideal world where the gap between REAL and IDEAL is at most $1/p(n)$ plus negligible. $p$ is any polynomial, so we can make the gap as small as we want, like $1/n^{100}$, just not negligible.

The important thing: the ideal world is the same fair ideal world as full security. Both parties always get correct output. They only relax the gap.

Point to the comparison table.

Three rows here. Full security: fair ideal, negligible gap, but impossible by Cleve. Security-with-abort: weakened ideal where the adversary can block, negligible gap, achievable but no fairness. $1/p$-security: fair ideal, $1/p$ gap. That's the sweet spot. We compare to the best possible world, just allow a little slack.

One thing to note: Katz proposed $1/p$-security-with-abort in STOC 2007, but that compares to the abort ideal, so it gives no fairness despite the name. Footnote 1 in the paper.

So to build a protocol for this, we need cover distributions.

The Definition: $\frac{1}{p}$-Security (Definition 1)

Definition 1: Protocol $\Pi$ $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$ if for every polynomial $p$ and every PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$, there exists a PPT simulator $\mathcal{S}$ in the complete fairness ideal world such that for all PPT distinguishers $D$:
$$\left|\Pr[D(\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)) = 1] - \Pr[D(\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)) = 1]\right| \leq \frac{1}{p(n)} + \mathrm{negl}(n)$$

Three notions compared

Notion Ideal world Gap Fairness?
Full security Fair (both get output) negligible Complete, but impossible
Security-with-abort Abort ($P_1$ can block $P_2$) negligible None
Definition 1 ($1/p$-security) Fair (both get output) $1/p(n)$ Partial
The ideal world is the same as for full security. The only relaxation is the gap. Since we compare to the fair ideal, the protocol is "almost as good" as a trusted party.
Contrast with Katz [STOC 07]: "$\frac{1}{p}$-security-with-abort" compares to the abort ideal. That gives no fairness despite the name (footnote 1 in the paper).

COVER DISTRIBUTIONS - ~1.5 min

Read the definition box.

Before the protocol, let me explain cover distributions. $D_1$ covers $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$ means: for every output $z$, $D_1(z) \geq \alpha \cdot D_2(z)$. $D_2$ is the real output, $D_1$ is the fake one used before the switch.

Why do we need this? The protocol switches from fake to real values. If some output has probability 0 under $D_1$ but positive under $D_2$, the adversary sees it and knows the switch happened. The cover condition stops that.

Point to the left column.

For Theorem 3, $|Y_n|$ is polynomial. $D_1$ picks a random $\hat{y}$ from $Y_n$ and outputs $f(x, \hat{y})$. Since $\hat{y}$ is uniform, any output $z$ has $D_1(z) \geq 1/|Y_n|$. And $D_2(z) \leq 1$. So $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$.

Point to the right column.

For Theorem 6, $|Y_n|$ can be super-poly. $f(x, \hat{y})$ alone might never produce some rare output $z$, so $D_1(z) = 0$ and the cover breaks. Fix: mix in a uniform component. With probability $1/p$ sample uniformly from $Z$, with $1 - 1/p$ sample $f(x, \hat{y})$. Now every $z$ has $D_1(z) \geq 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$, so $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$. Smaller $\alpha$, so we need more rounds.

Point to the bottom box.

Bigger $\alpha$ means harder for the adversary to detect the switch. Lemma 2 says abort probability is at most $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$. We pick $r$ so this equals $1/p$. For Theorem 3 that's $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$. For Theorem 6 it's $r = p^2 \cdot |Z|$, quadratic because $\alpha$ is worse.

Cover Distributions

Distribution $D_1$ (fake/cover) over output space $Z$ covers $D_2$ (real output distribution) with parameter $\alpha$ if for every $z \in Z$: $$\Pr_{a \leftarrow D_1}[a = z] \geq \alpha \cdot \Pr_{a \leftarrow D_2}[a = z]$$

$D_2$ is the real output distribution (i.e. $f(x,y)$), and $D_1$ is the fake distribution used before the switch. Every value possible under $D_2$ must be reasonably likely under $D_1$, otherwise the adversary detects the switch.

Theorem 3 (poly-size domain)

  • $|Y_n|$ is polynomial
  • $D_1$: pick uniform $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$, output $f(x, \hat{y})$
  • At least one $\hat{y}$ maps to each reachable $z$
    $\Rightarrow D_1(z) \geq 1/|Y_n|$
  • Since $D_2(z) \leq 1$:
    $D_1(z) \geq \frac{1}{|Y_n|} \cdot D_2(z)$
  • $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$
  • Rounds: $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$

Theorem 6 (poly-size range)

  • $|Y_n|$ super-poly, $|Z|$ polynomial
  • $f(x,\hat{y})$ alone fails: $D_1(z) \approx 0$ for rare $z$
  • $D_1$: mix $\mathrm{Uniform}(Z)$ (prob $1/p$) and $f$
  • Uniform part guarantees for all $z$:
    $D_1(z) \geq \frac{1}{p} \cdot \frac{1}{|Z|}$
  • Since $D_2(z) \leq 1$:
    $D_1(z) \geq \frac{1}{p \cdot |Z|} \cdot D_2(z)$
  • $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$
  • Rounds: $r = p^2 \cdot |Z|$

THE PROTOCOL - ~2 min

Point to the left column.

The protocol has two phases. Phase 1 is setup. The parties run a secure-with-abort protocol for this helper called ShareGen.

ShareGen picks a secret switch point $i^*$ uniform from 1 to $r$. Before $i^*$, it makes fake values from the cover distribution. From $i^*$ on, it uses the real output $f(x,y)$. Then it XOR-splits each value into two halves, makes MAC keys and tags, and gives each party their halves.

Point to the right column.

Phase 2 is the actual exchange. Each round, $P_2$ sends first: its share half with a MAC tag. $P_1$ checks the MAC, XORs the halves, gets $a_i$. Then $P_1$ sends to $P_2$, same thing. At the end each party outputs the last value they verified.

Important: $P_2$ sends first every round, so $P_1$ is always one step ahead. That's why the proof handles corrupt $P_1$ and corrupt $P_2$ separately.

The MACs stop the adversary from sending fake shares. Bad MAC means we ignore it.

Now the security of all this comes down to one lemma.

The Protocol (Section 3)

Phase 1: Setup via $\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}$

Run any secure-with-abort protocol for $\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}(x,y)$.

  1. Pick switch point $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$ uniformly
  2. For $i < i^*$: sample $a_i \leftarrow D_1$, $b_i \leftarrow D'_1$ (cover distribution)
  3. For $i \geq i^*$: set $a_i = f^1(x,y)$, $b_i = f^2(x,y)$ (real output)
  4. XOR-split each value: $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$, same for $b_i$
  5. Generate two MAC keys $k_a, k_b$. Tags: $t_i^a = \mathrm{Mac}_{k_a}(i \| a_i^{(2)})$, $t_i^b = \mathrm{Mac}_{k_b}(i \| b_i^{(1)})$
  6. Give $P_1$: $a_i^{(1)}$, $(b_i^{(1)}, t_i^b)$, and key $k_a$
  7. Give $P_2$: $(a_i^{(2)}, t_i^a)$, $b_i^{(2)}$, and key $k_b$

Phase 2: Gradual release

For each round $i = 1, \ldots, r$:

  1. $P_2$ sends $(a_i^{(2)},\;\mathrm{tag})$ to $P_1$
  2. $P_1$ verifies MAC. If valid: $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$
  3. $P_1$ sends $(b_i^{(1)},\;\mathrm{tag})$ to $P_2$
  4. $P_2$ verifies MAC. If valid: $b_i = b_i^{(1)} \oplus b_i^{(2)}$

Each party outputs the last successfully verified value.

Asymmetry

$P_2$ sends first each round, so $P_1$ has a one-round information lead. The proof handles malicious $P_1$ (Claim 4) and malicious $P_2$ (Claim 5) separately.

Why MACs?

Prevent the adversary from sending modified share halves. If a MAC check fails, the received message is simply ignored.

LEMMA 2 - ~2 min

Most technical slide. Don't rush.

Read the Lemma 2 statement and formula box.

This is the heart of the paper. Lemma 2 says: if $D_1$ covers $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$, and the switch point $i^*$ is uniform in 1 to $r$, the adversary sees $D_1$ values before the switch and $D_2$ values after. Then no matter what strategy the adversary uses, it aborts at exactly $i^*$ with probability at most $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$.

Proof is by induction on $r$. Base case $r = 1$ is easy. For the inductive step, we prove for $r+1$. WLOG the adversary is deterministic, so its strategy is a set $S$: it aborts iff $v_1 \in S$.

Point to the left column.

If $i^* = 1$, which happens with probability $1/(r+1)$, then $v_1$ comes from $D_2$. The adversary aborts if $v_1 \in S$, so the win probability from this case is $(1/(r+1)) \cdot \Pr_{D_2}[v_1 \in S]$.

Point to the right column.

If $i^* > 1$, which is probability $r/(r+1)$, then $v_1$ comes from $D_1$. If $v_1 \notin S$, the adversary continues, and the remaining $r$ rounds are the same game. By induction, the win probability is at most $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$.

Point to the derivation box at the bottom.

When we combine both cases and apply the cover property, the $\Pr_{D_2}[S]$ terms cancel out, and we get exactly $1/(\alpha(r+1))$. That's the key step.

Now let me show how the simulator uses this.

Lemma 2: Bounding the Abort Probability

Lemma 2: Let $D_1$ cover $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$, and let $i^*$ be uniform in $\{1,\ldots,r\}$. The adversary observes $v_i \sim D_1$ for $i < i^*$ and $v_i \sim D_2$ for $i \geq i^*$. For any adaptive abort strategy:
$$\Pr[\text{adversary aborts at exactly } i^*] \leq \frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r}$$

($v_i$ is just the value the adversary sees in round $i$. It's $a_i$ if $P_1$ is corrupt, or $b_i$ if $P_2$ is corrupt.)

Proof (induction on $r$)

  • Base case ($r=1$): $i^* = 1$ always. $\Pr[\text{abort at } i^*] \leq 1 \leq 1/\alpha = 1/(\alpha \cdot 1)$ since $\alpha \leq 1$.
  • Inductive step: Assume true for $r$, prove for $r+1$. WLOG adversary is deterministic. Its strategy is a set $S \subseteq Z$: abort iff $v_1 \in S$.

Case: $i^* = 1$ (prob $\frac{1}{r+1}$)

  • $v_1 \sim D_2$ (real distribution)
  • Adversary aborts if $v_1 \in S$
  • $\Pr[\text{win}] = \frac{1}{r+1}\Pr_{D_2}[v_1 \in S]$

Case: $i^* > 1$ (prob $\frac{r}{r+1}$)

  • $v_1 \sim D_1$ (cover distribution)
  • Adversary continues if $v_1 \notin S$
  • Remaining rounds $\equiv \Gamma(\alpha, r)$
  • By induction: $\Pr[\text{win}] \leq \frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r}$
Total Win Prob: $$\begin{aligned} &\leq \frac{1}{r+1}\Pr_{D_2}[S] + \frac{r}{r+1}(1 - \Pr_{D_1}[S])\frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r} \\ &= \frac{1}{r+1}\Pr_{D_2}[S] + \frac{1}{\alpha(r+1)}(1 - \Pr_{D_1}[S]) \\ &\leq \frac{1}{r+1}\Pr_{D_2}[S] + \frac{1}{\alpha(r+1)}(1 - \alpha\Pr_{D_2}[S]) \quad \text{(by cover property)} \\ &= \frac{1}{r+1}\Pr_{D_2}[S] + \frac{1}{\alpha(r+1)} - \frac{1}{r+1}\Pr_{D_2}[S] \\ &= \frac{1}{\alpha(r+1)} \quad \square \end{aligned}$$

SIMULATOR - ~1.5 min

Point to the left column.

Read the goal box for Claim 4.

Here's the simulator for corrupt $P_1$, Claim 4. We build $\mathcal{S}_1$ in the fair ideal world so IDEAL and HYBRID are $1/p$-close. It works like this: extract the adversary's input $x'$ from Phase 1. Pick $i^*$. Before $i^*$, we don't know $y$, so pick random $\hat{y}$, compute $f^1(x', \hat{y})$, split and send. At $i^*$, send $x'$ to the trusted party, get back $z_1 = f^1(x', y)$. From then on everything is real.

The gap: if the adversary aborts at exactly $i^*$, $P_2$ gets output based on random $\hat{x}$ instead of real $x$. But Lemma 2 says this happens with probability at most $1/p$. If it aborts after $i^*$, both have the correct answer, no gap.

Point to the right column.

Claim 5 for corrupt $P_2$ is actually simpler. Since $P_2$ sends first every round, $P_1$ always gets its value before $P_2$ can abort. So $P_1$ never ends up with a wrong answer. Complete fairness holds, zero gap.

Point to the box at the bottom.

Privacy is free. The adversary sees the same XOR shares and MACs in both worlds. Only the honest party's output differs, and that's bounded by $1/p$ plus negligible.

Now let me state the main results.

Simulator Construction

Claim 4: Malicious $P_1$

Goal: Construct $\mathcal{S}_1$ in fair ideal world s.t. $\{\mathsf{IDEAL}\} \stackrel{1/p}{\approx} \{\mathsf{HYBRID}\}$
  1. Extract input: Run Phase 1 simulator, get $\mathcal{A}$'s input $x'$
  2. Pick $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$
  3. Rounds $i < i^*$: Don't know $y$. Pick $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$, compute $a_i=f^1(x', \hat{y})$. XOR-split and send to $\mathcal{A}$
  4. Round $i^*$: Send $x'$ to trusted party. Receive $z_1 = f^1(x', y)$
  5. Rounds $i \geq i^*$: Set $a_i = z_1$. Simulation is perfect

Gap: If $\mathcal{A}$ aborts at $i^*$, honest $P_2$ outputs $f(\hat{x}, y)$ (wrong) in hybrid, but $f(x, y)$ (correct) in ideal. By Lemma 2, abort at $i^*$ happens with $\Pr \leq 1/(\alpha r) = 1/p$.

Claim 5: Malicious $P_2$

Goal: Construct $\mathcal{S}_2$ in fair ideal world s.t. $\{\mathsf{IDEAL}\} \equiv \{\mathsf{HYBRID}\}$ (complete fairness)
  1. Extract input: Get $\mathcal{A}$'s input $y'$
  2. Symmetric to Claim 4, but simpler
  3. $P_1$ always receives its share first in every round
  4. So $P_1$ always gets the correct output before $P_2$ can abort

No gap at all: $P_1$ gets output first in every round, so a malicious $P_2$ can never cause $P_1$ to get a wrong answer. Complete fairness holds.

Privacy is immediate: $\mathcal{A}$'s view is identical in both worlds (same XOR shares, same MACs). The only difference is the honest party's output, bounded by $1/p + \mathrm{negl}(n)$.

THEOREM 3 - ~1 min

Read the theorem statement.

First feasibility result. If one input domain is poly-size, say $|Y_n|$ grows polynomially in $n$, then for every polynomial $p$ we get an $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$-round protocol that gives us $1/p$-security, privacy, and with a small tweak, security-with-abort too. Assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Proof sketch: use gradual release with $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$ rounds. Cover distribution is $a_i = f(x, \hat{y})$ for random $\hat{y}$, giving $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$. Lemma 2 gives abort probability $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = |Y_n|/(p \cdot |Y_n|) = 1/p$. Security from Claims 4 and 5. Privacy is free from XOR shares.

Bonus: we also get security-with-abort. Pick $i^*$ from $\{2, \ldots, r+1\}$ instead, set $b_{i^*-1} = \bot$. Corrupt $P_2$ can detect $i^*$, but $P_1$ already has the output by then so it doesn't matter.

Point to the bottom grid.

Round complexity: upper bound $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$, lower bound $\Omega(\min\{p, |X_n|, |Y_n|\})$ from Section 5. For binary output it's just $O(p)$. The assumption is enhanced trapdoor permutations, which give us OT, enough for Yao or GMW. Nothing exotic.

What about when only the output range is poly-size?

Theorem 3: Polynomial-Size Domain (Section 3.2)

Theorem 3: If $|Y_n| = \mathrm{poly}(n)$ (or symmetrically $|X_n|$), then for every polynomial $p$, there exists an $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$-round protocol that $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$, privately computes $f$, and (with a small modification) securely-with-abort computes $f$, assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Proof sketch

  • Protocol: Use the gradual release construction with $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$ rounds and $\mathsf{ShareGen}_r$
  • Cover distribution: $a_i = f(x, \hat{y})$ for $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$. Then $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$
  • Security bound: By Lemma 2, $\Pr[\text{abort at } i^*] \leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r) = |Y_n|/(p \cdot |Y_n|) = 1/p$
  • $1/p$-security follows from Claims 4 (malicious $P_1$) and 5 (malicious $P_2$)
  • Privacy: Adversary's view is simulatable (XOR shares are uniformly distributed)
  • Security-with-abort: Modify by choosing $i^* \leftarrow \{2,\ldots,r+1\}$, set $b_{i^*-1} = \bot$

Round complexity

Upper bound: $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$ (Theorem 3)

Lower bound: $\Omega(\min\{p, |X_n|, |Y_n|\})$ (Section 5)

For binary output: $O(p)$ rounds. Gap remains for general case.

Assumption

Enhanced trapdoor permutations (imply OT, sufficient for general 2PC via Yao/GMW). No random oracles or non-standard assumptions.

THEOREM 6 - ~1.5 min

Read the theorem statement.

Now the harder case. If one output range $|Z^1_n|$ is polynomial, we get $O(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|)$ rounds for $1/p$-security and privacy. Same assumption. But no security-with-abort this time.

Point to the left column.

Big change: the cover distribution. We use a mix. With probability $1/p$, pick $a_i$ uniform from $Z^1_n$. With probability $1 - 1/p$, compute $a_i = f^1(x, \hat{y})$ with random $\hat{y}$. This makes every output have probability at least $1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$, so $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$.

Point to the right column.

But we can't get security-with-abort. If corrupt $P_2$ aborts before $i^*$, $P_1$ outputs $a_i$ from this mixed distribution. That distribution differs from the ideal by $O(1/p)$. Fine for $1/p$-security, but abort security needs negligible gap. Theorems 9 and 11 show this is inherent, not just a proof weakness.

Point to the bottom grid.

Rounds: $r = p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|$. Lemma 2 gives $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = (p \cdot |Z^1_n|)/(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|) = 1/p$. Security: Claim 7 for corrupt $P_1$ is same as before, Claim 8 for corrupt $P_2$ has gap at most $1/p$.

Now let me show these bounds are tight.

Theorem 6: Polynomial-Size Range (Section 3.3)

Theorem 6: If $|Z^1_n| = \mathrm{poly}(n)$ (or symmetrically $|Z^2_n|$), then for every polynomial $p$, there exists an $O(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|)$-round protocol that $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$ and privately computes $f$, assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Key changes from Theorem 3

Modified cover distribution ($\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}$)

  • With prob $1/p$: $a_i \leftarrow \mathrm{Uniform}(Z^1_n)$
  • With prob $1 - 1/p$: $a_i = f^1(x, \hat{y})$ for $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$
  • Guarantees $\Pr[a_i = z] \geq \frac{1}{p \cdot |Z^1_n|}$ for all $z$
  • So $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$

Why no security-with-abort?

  • If malicious $P_2$ aborts before $i^*$: $P_1$ outputs $a_i$ from the mixed distribution
  • Real-world distribution of $a_i$ differs from ideal by $O(1/p)$
  • This gap is tolerable for $1/p$-security but prevents achieving the abort ideal
  • Theorems 9 and 11 show this is inherent

Round complexity

Rounds: $r = p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|$

Bound: $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = \frac{p \cdot |Z^1_n|}{p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|} = 1/p$

Security

Claim 7 (malicious $P_1$): same as Theorem 3
Claim 8 (malicious $P_2$): statistical gap $\leq 1/p$

THEOREM 9 - ~2 min

Take this one slow, it's the key impossibility result.

Theorem 9 is about equality on strings of length $\ell(n) = \omega(\log n)$, so both domains are super-poly. It says: for $p \geq 4$, you can't have $1/p$-security and security-with-abort at the same time.

Proof uses three experiments. Random: pick $x, y$ independently, so EQ = 0 with high probability. Equal: pick $x$ uniform, set $y = x$, so EQ = 1 always. Mixed: flip coin $c$, heads do random, tails do equal.

Point to the Claim 10 box.

Claim 10 uses security-with-abort to show that in the random experiment, the probability any $a_i$ equals 1 is negligible. If it weren't, an adversary could abort at a random round and make $P_1$ output 1 when the answer is 0.

The adversary is simple: corrupt $P_1$, run honestly, check $a_i$ each round. If $a_i = 1$, abort and guess $c = 1$. Otherwise finish and guess $c = 0$.

Point to the ideal world and real world boxes.

In the ideal world, the adversary guesses correctly with probability at most $1/2$. In the real world, it's at least $3/4$ minus negligible. Gap is $1/4$. That kills $1/p$-security for $p \geq 4$.

Theorem 11 goes further and doesn't even need security-with-abort.

Theorem 9: Impossibility for Equality (Section 4.1)

Theorem 9: Let $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$ be equality on strings of length $\ell(n) = \omega(\log n)$. For $p \geq 4$, no protocol can simultaneously $\frac{1}{p}$-securely and securely-with-abort compute $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$.

Proof: Three experiments

  1. Random experiment ($\Pr_{\mathrm{rand}}$): $x, y \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\ell(n)}$ independently. $\mathrm{EQ} = 0$ w.h.p.
  2. Equal experiment ($\Pr_{\mathrm{eq}}$): $x \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\ell(n)}$, set $y = x$. $\mathrm{EQ} = 1$ always.
  3. Mixed experiment: Flip coin $c$. If $c=0$: random. If $c=1$: equal.
Claim 10 (from security-with-abort): $\Pr_{\mathrm{rand}}[a_0 = 1 \lor \cdots \lor a_r = 1]$ is $\mathrm{negl}(n)$. Otherwise a random-round aborter would cause $P_1$ to output 1 in the real world with non-negl probability, contradicting the abort ideal where $\mathrm{EQ}=0$ w.h.p.

The adversary $\mathcal{A}$ (corrupting $P_1$)

Run $\Pi$ honestly, compute $a_i$ after each round. If $a_i = 1$: abort, output "guess $c=1$". If never: complete protocol, output "guess $c=0$".

Eq (2): Ideal world

$\Pr^{\mathrm{ideal}}_3[\mathrm{guess} \wedge \mathrm{out}_2 \neq 1] \leq \frac{1}{2}$

Eq (3): Real world

$\Pr^{\mathrm{real}}_3[\mathrm{guess} \wedge \mathrm{out}_2 \neq 1]$

$\geq \frac{1}{2}(1-\mathrm{negl}) + \frac{1}{2}\Pr_{\mathrm{eq}}[i^* \leq j^*]$

$\geq \frac{3}{4} - \mathrm{negl}(n)$

Gap $\geq 1/4$. Rules out $1/p$-security for $p \geq 4$.

THEOREM 11 - ~1.5 min

This is the strongest impossibility. There exists a function that can't be $1/p$-securely computed for any $p \geq 2$. And this doesn't need security-with-abort. Just $1/p$-security alone is impossible.

The function is Swap with embedded MACs. $P_1$ has its value $x_1$, a tag $t_1$, a key $k_2$ to check the other party, and extra key-tag pair $(k'_2, t'_2)$. Output is $(x_1, x_2)$ if MACs verify, $\bot$ otherwise.

The extra key-tag pairs are the trick. The functionality ignores them, but the adversary uses them during the protocol to check if the value it's receiving is correct.

Proof: in the ideal world, $P_1$ can't win, can't get $x_2$ while blocking $P_2$, because the trusted party gives both at once. But the adversary $P^*_1$ runs honestly until some round where the MAC verifies on $a_i$. Since it's an IT-MAC, that means $a_i = x_2$ with overwhelming probability. So $P_1$ grabs it and aborts.

Let $i$ be the first round $P_1$ can verify, $j$ the first round $P_2$ can verify. One goes first, so $\Pr[i \leq j] + \Pr[j < i] = 1$. At least one of $P^*_1, P^*_2$ wins with probability at least $1/2$. In the ideal world it's negligible. Gap is $1/2$. Rules out $p \geq 2$.

The bottom box explains why IT-MACs specifically: computational MACs allow forgery by strong adversaries.

Let me put everything together now.

Theorem 11: Impossibility for Swap (Section 4.2)

Theorem 11: There exists a functionality that cannot be $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computed for $p \geq 2$.

The Swap function with embedded MACs

$P_1$ input: $(x_1,\; t_1,\; k_2,\; k'_2,\; t'_2)$   $P_2$ input: $(x_2,\; t_2,\; k_1,\; k'_1,\; t'_1)$

$$\mathrm{Swap}_n = \begin{cases} (x_1, x_2) & \text{if } \mathrm{Vrfy}_{k_1}(x_1, t_1) = \mathrm{Vrfy}_{k_2}(x_2, t_2) = 1 \\ \bot & \text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

$(k'_i, t'_i)$: extra MAC key/tag on $x_i$ under key $k'_i$. Ignored by functionality, used by adversary to verify.

Proof

  • Ideal world: $P_1$ cannot "win" (get $x_2$ while $P_2$ doesn't get $x_1$).
  • Adversary $P^*_1$: Run honestly until first round $i$ where $\mathrm{Vrfy}_{k'_2}(a_i, t'_2) = 1$. Then $a_i = x_2$ (by IT-MAC security). Output $a_i$ and abort.
  • Let $i$ = first round $P_1$ can verify $x_2$,   $j$ = first round $P_2$ can verify $x_1$.
  • $\Pr[i \leq j] + \Pr[j > i] = 1$   $\Rightarrow$   one of $P^*_1, P^*_2$ wins with $\Pr \geq 1/2 - \mathrm{negl}(n)$
  • Ideal-world win: $\mathrm{negl}(n)$.   Gap $\geq 1/2$. Rules out $p \geq 2$.
Why IT-MAC? The proof is statistical. A computationally-secure MAC allows forgery by powerful adversaries.

COMPLETE PICTURE - ~1 min

Walk through the table row by row.

Putting it all together. Theorem 3: poly-size domain gives us everything, $1/p$-security, abort, privacy. Theorem 6: poly-size range gives $1/p$-security and privacy, but not abort. Theorem 9: super-poly domains like equality, can't combine $1/p$-security with abort for $p \geq 4$. Theorem 11: everything super-poly like Swap, can't even get $1/p$-security for $p \geq 2$.

So feasibility and impossibility match exactly at the polynomial-size boundary. Partial fairness works if and only if at least one domain or range is polynomial. That's a tight result and it answers Goldreich's open problem.

Point to the key dependencies below.

Both feasibility results need enhanced trapdoor permutations. Theorem 9 needs both $1/p$-security and abort together. Theorem 11 only needs $1/p$-security, so it's a stronger impossibility.

OPEN QUESTIONS - ~1 min

Three open problems from Section 5.

First, fair exchange of digital signatures. Theorem 11 rules this out since exchanging signatures is basically Swap. So what's the right definition of partial fairness for that?

Second, round complexity gap. Upper bound is $O(p^2 \cdot |Z|)$, lower is $\Omega(\min\{p, |X|, |Y|\})$. For binary output that's $O(p^2)$ vs $\Omega(p)$, a quadratic gap. Can we close it?

Third, multi-party partial fairness with dishonest majority. Almost completely open beyond coin tossing. Lemma 2 is fundamentally two-party.

Let me wrap up.

Open Questions (Section 5)

1. Fair exchange of signatures: Theorem 11 rules out $\frac{1}{p}$-security for Swap. Exchanging digital signatures is a form of Swap. What is the right notion of partial fairness for this setting?
2. Round complexity gap: Upper bound $O(p^2 \cdot |Z|)$ vs. lower bound $\Omega(\min\{p, |X|, |Y|\})$. For binary output: $O(p^2)$ vs. $\Omega(p)$. Can the lower bound be improved to $\Omega(p^2)$, or can a protocol achieve $O(p)$ rounds?
3. Multi-party with dishonest majority: Almost entirely open beyond coin tossing. General partial fairness with $t \geq n/2$ corruptions is unknown. The Lemma 2 approach is fundamentally two-party.

CLOSING - ~30 sec

So to sum up: Gordon and Katz gave a clean definition of partial fairness, $1/p$-security, and showed it works exactly when at least one domain or range is poly-size. Their protocol hits the $1/p$ bound, and the impossibility results for equality and Swap show the boundary is tight. This settles Goldreich's open problem about partial fairness.
