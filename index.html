OPENING - ~1 min

Good morning everyone. Today I will be presenting the paper Partial Fairness in Secure Two-Party Computation by S. Dov Gordon and Jonathan Katz, published at Eurocrypt 2010.

Cleve showed in 1986 that complete fairness is impossible for general two-party computation. Since then, the standard approach has been security-with-abort, which provides privacy and correctness but zero fairness guarantee.

Gordon and Katz asked a simple but powerful question: can we get something in between? Their answer is yes, for a well-defined class of functions, and they prove their results are tight.

BACKGROUND - ~1.5 min

This is the standard two-party computation setting since Yao's work in 1986. Two parties, $P_1$ and $P_2$, hold private inputs $x$ and $y$. They want to jointly compute a function $f(x,y)$ without revealing their inputs to each other.

Security is formalized using the real-ideal paradigm. In the real world, the parties run a protocol and the adversary controls one party. In the ideal world, a trusted third party does the computation honestly.

A protocol is secure if for every real-world adversary, there exists an ideal-world simulator whose output is computationally indistinguishable.

Point to the table.

We want four properties. The first three - privacy, correctness, and input independence - have been achievable since Yao. Fairness is the hard one. Let me explain why.

Secure Two-Party Computation

Two parties $P_1$ (input $x \in X_n$) and $P_2$ (input $y \in Y_n$) jointly compute a functionality $f_n : X_n \times Y_n \to Z^1_n \times Z^2_n$. Security is defined via the real/ideal paradigm.

Real world

No trusted party. Parties execute protocol $\Pi$. Adversary $\mathcal{A}$ controls one party, sends arbitrary messages, can abort at any time.

Output: $\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)$ = joint output of $\mathcal{A}$ and the honest party.

Ideal world

A trusted party receives inputs, computes $f(x,y)$, delivers outputs. Adversary $\mathcal{S}$ (simulator) can only interact with the trusted party.

Output: $\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)$ = joint output of $\mathcal{S}$ and the honest party.

Security properties

PropertyMeaningStatus
PrivacyAdversary's view is simulatable from output aloneAchievable [Yao 86]
CorrectnessHonest party always gets $f^i(x,y)$Achievable
Input independenceAdversary commits input before seeing honest party's inputAchievable
FairnessBoth get output, or neither doesImpossible [Cleve 86]

CLEVE'S IMPOSSIBILITY - ~1.5 min

This is Cleve's result from 1986, which is the starting point for this entire paper.

Consider any $r$-round protocol. Define $a_i$ as $P_1$'s output if $P_2$ aborts after round $i$. At round zero, nobody knows the output. At round $r$, both parties know it. So somewhere in between, there must be a threshold round where one party first learns the answer before the other.

The adversary can be the party that crosses this threshold first. It detects the transition, aborts immediately, and walks away with the correct answer while the other party does not have it. Cleve showed the advantage is $\Omega(1/r)$.

After this result, the entire field adopted security-with-abort: the adversary receives its output and can then block the honest party. This gives zero fairness. Goldreich flagged in his textbook that defining partial fairness properly was an open problem.

Gordon and Katz's key insight is how to define partial fairness in the right way.

Cleve's Impossibility (1986)

Theorem [Cleve 86]: For any $r$-round two-party coin-flipping protocol, a malicious adversary can bias the outcome by $\Omega(1/r)$. Complete fairness is impossible for general 2PC without honest majority.

The argument (intuition)

  • Let $a_i$ = $P_1$'s output if $P_2$ aborts after round $i$;   $b_i$ = $P_2$'s output if $P_1$ aborts after round $i$
  • At round 0: nobody knows $f(x,y)$.   At round $r$: both know $f(x,y)$
  • There exists a threshold round where one party's output transitions from "unknown" to "known" before the other's
  • That party can abort at the threshold and walk away with the answer
  • Advantage: $\Omega(1/r)$ (tight: Moran-Naor-Segev 2009 showed $O(1/r)$ is achievable for coin tossing)

Consequence: Security-with-abort (Def 13)

How it works

  • Trusted party computes $f(x,y) = (z_1, z_2)$
  • Sends $z_1$ to $P_1$ first
  • $P_1$ then sends continue or abort
  • If abort: trusted party sends $\bot$ to $P_2$

Properties

  • Privacy: preserved (negligible gap)
  • Correctness: preserved
  • Input independence: preserved
  • Fairness: none

Goldreich (Section 7.7.1.1): defining partial fairness was an open problem.

THE DEFINITION - ~2 min

This is the most important conceptual slide. Slow down here.

The paper's main conceptual contribution is what they call Definition 1 in Section 2 of the paper - I refer to it as Def 3 following the numbering in my report. Let me unpack it carefully.

The ideal world in this definition is the same fair ideal world used for complete security - both parties always get the correct output. The only relaxation is the distinguishing gap: we allow $1/p(n)$ instead of requiring it to be negligible.

Point to the comparison table.

Look at these three notions side by side. Full security uses the fair ideal world with a negligible gap - but it is impossible by Cleve. Security-with-abort weakens the ideal world to allow blocking, with a negligible gap - achievable, but no fairness. Gordon and Katz's $1/p$-security keeps the fair ideal world but allows a $1/p$ gap. This is the sweet spot.

An important subtlety: Katz earlier proposed $1/p$-security-with-abort in his STOC 2007 paper, which compares to the abort ideal. That gives no fairness at all despite the name. The paper makes this point in footnote 1.

Now, to build a protocol achieving this definition, we need a tool called a cover distribution.

The Definition: $\frac{1}{p}$-Security (Def 3)

Definition 3: Protocol $\Pi$ $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$ if for every PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$, there exists a PPT simulator $\mathcal{S}$ in the complete fairness ideal world such that for all PPT distinguishers $D$:
$$\left|\Pr[D(\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)) = 1] - \Pr[D(\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)) = 1]\right| \leq \frac{1}{p(n)} + \mathrm{negl}(n)$$

Three notions compared

NotionIdeal worldGapFairness?
Def 12 (full security)Fair (both get output)negligibleComplete, but impossible
Def 13 (security-with-abort)Abort ($P_1$ can block $P_2$)negligibleNone
Def 3 ($1/p$-security)Fair (both get output)$1/p(n)$Partial
Key insight: The ideal world is the same as for full security. The only relaxation is the gap. Since we compare to the fair ideal, the protocol is "almost as good" as a trusted party.
Contrast with Katz [STOC 07]: "$\frac{1}{p}$-security-with-abort" compares to the abort ideal. That gives no fairness despite the name (footnote 1 in the paper).

COVER DISTRIBUTIONS - ~1 min

A cover distribution $D_1$ covers $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$ if every value that is possible under $D_2$ is also reasonably likely under $D_1$. Formally, $D_1(z) \geq \alpha \cdot D_2(z)$ for every $z$.

The intuition is simple: if the adversary ever sees a value that is impossible under the cover distribution but possible under the real distribution, it immediately knows the switch has happened. The cover prevents this.

The simplest choice is the uniform distribution over $Z$, which gives $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$.

Point to the formula at the bottom.

Why does $\alpha$ matter? Because the key lemma - Lemma 2 - will bound the adversary's advantage by $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$. Plugging in $\alpha = 1/(p|Z|)$ and $r = p^2|Z|$ gives exactly $1/p$. That is the origin of the security bound.

With this in hand, let me describe the protocol itself.

Cover Distributions

Definition: Distribution $D_1$ over $Z$ covers $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$ if for every $z \in Z$: $$\Pr_{a \leftarrow D_1}[a = z] \geq \alpha \cdot \Pr_{a \leftarrow D_2}[a = z]$$

Intuitively: every value possible under $D_2$ (real output) must be reasonably likely under $D_1$ (fake output). Otherwise the adversary could detect the switch by seeing an "impossible" value.

Choice 1: Uniform distribution

  • $D_1 = \mathrm{Uniform}(Z)$
  • $D_1(z) = 1/|Z|$, and $D_2(z) \leq 1$
  • So $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$ works
  • Used in Theorem 3 (poly-size range)

Choice 2: Mixed distribution

  • With prob $1/p$: sample $\mathrm{Uniform}(Z)$
  • With prob $1 - 1/p$: sample from $D_2$ itself
  • Same $\alpha$, but $D_1$ is much closer to $D_2$
  • Used in Theorem 6 (poly-size domain)

Why $\alpha$ matters

Lemma 2 will show that the adversary's probability of detecting the switch point is $\leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r)$. With $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$ and $r = p^2 \cdot |Z|$:

$$\frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r} = \frac{p \cdot |Z|}{p^2 \cdot |Z|} = \frac{1}{p}$$

THE PROTOCOL - ~2 min

The protocol has two phases. Phase 1 is setup. The parties run a standard secure-with-abort protocol to jointly compute a helper functionality called ShareGen-prime.

ShareGen picks a secret switch point $i^*$ uniformly from 1 to $r$. For each round before $i^*$, it generates fake values sampled from the cover distribution. From round $i^*$ onward, it uses the real output $f(x,y)$. Every value is XOR-split into two halves and authenticated with information-theoretic MACs.

Phase 2 is gradual release. In each round, parties exchange their half-shares with MAC tags. The receiver verifies the MAC, XORs both halves to reconstruct the value, and stores it. At the end, each party outputs the last successfully verified value.

There is an important asymmetry here: $P_2$ sends first in every round, which means $P_1$ always has a one-round information lead. This is why the security proof handles malicious $P_1$ and malicious $P_2$ as separate cases - Claims 4 and 5 for Theorem 3, and Claims 7 and 8 for Theorem 6.

The security of this entire construction rests on one key lemma.

The Protocol (Section 3)

Phase 1: Setup via $\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}$

Run any secure-with-abort protocol for $\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}(x,y)$.

  1. Pick switch point $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$ uniformly
  2. For $i < i^*$: sample $a_i \leftarrow D_1$, $b_i \leftarrow D'_1$ (cover distribution)
  3. For $i \geq i^*$: set $a_i = f^1(x,y)$, $b_i = f^2(x,y)$ (real output)
  4. XOR-split each value: $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$
  5. Generate MAC keys $k_i$ and tags $t_i = \mathrm{Mac}_{k_i}(a_i^{(j)})$
  6. Give $P_1$ its halves $\{a_i^{(1)}, b_i^{(1)}\}$ + MAC tags/keys
  7. Give $P_2$ its halves $\{a_i^{(2)}, b_i^{(2)}\}$ + MAC tags/keys

Phase 2: Gradual release

For each round $i = 1, \ldots, r$:

  1. $P_2$ sends $(a_i^{(2)},\;\mathrm{tag})$ to $P_1$
  2. $P_1$ verifies MAC. If valid: $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$
  3. $P_1$ sends $(b_i^{(1)},\;\mathrm{tag})$ to $P_2$
  4. $P_2$ verifies MAC. If valid: $b_i = b_i^{(1)} \oplus b_i^{(2)}$

Each party outputs the last successfully verified value.

Asymmetry

$P_2$ sends first each round, so $P_1$ has a one-round information lead. The proof handles malicious $P_1$ (Claim 4) and malicious $P_2$ (Claim 5) separately.

Why MACs?

Prevent the adversary from sending modified share halves. If a MAC check fails, the received message is simply ignored.

LEMMA 2 - ~2 min

This is the most technical slide. Take your time.

This is the heart of the paper. The adversary observes a sequence of values $v_1$ through $v_r$. Before the switch point $i^*$, each value comes from the cover distribution $D_1$. From $i^*$ onward, each value comes from the real distribution $D_2$. The adversary adaptively decides when to abort.

Lemma 2 says: no matter what strategy the adversary uses, the probability that it aborts at exactly $i^*$ is at most $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$.

The proof is by induction on $r$. The base case $r = 1$ is trivial. For the inductive step: after observing $v_1$, the adversary's strategy is a function $\sigma$ from $Z$ to the interval $[0,1]$, representing the probability of aborting.

Point to the left column.

If $i^* = 1$, the adversary sees a value from $D_2$ and aborts with probability $\sigma(v_1)$. The hit probability is $(1/r) \sum_z D_2(z) \sigma(z)$. But the cover property constrains this - $D_1(z) \geq \alpha \cdot D_2(z)$ - so the adversary cannot concentrate its aborting too heavily on values that distinguish the distributions.

Point to the right column.

If $i^*$ is greater than 1, $v_1$ comes from $D_1$, and the remaining rounds form a subgame handled by the inductive hypothesis.

Now I will show how the simulator uses this lemma.

Lemma 2: Bounding the Abort Probability

Lemma 2: Let $D_1$ cover $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$, and let $i^*$ be uniform in $\{1,\ldots,r\}$. The adversary observes $v_i \sim D_1$ for $i < i^*$ and $v_i \sim D_2$ for $i \geq i^*$. For any adaptive abort strategy:
$$\Pr[\text{adversary aborts at exactly } i^*] \leq \frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r}$$

Proof (induction on $r$)

  • Base case ($r=1$): $i^* = 1$ always. $\Pr[\text{abort at } i^*] \leq 1 \leq 1/\alpha = 1/(\alpha \cdot 1)$ since $\alpha \leq 1$.
  • Inductive step: After observing $v_1$, the adversary's strategy is $\sigma: Z \to [0,1]$ (probability of aborting).

Case: $i^* = 1$ (prob $1/r$)

$v_1 \sim D_2$. Adversary aborts with prob $\sigma(v_1)$.

$\Pr[\text{hit}] = \frac{1}{r}\sum_z D_2(z) \cdot \sigma(z)$

Cover property constrains this: $D_1(z) \geq \alpha \cdot D_2(z)$, so $\sigma$ cannot concentrate on values more likely under $D_2$.

Case: $i^* > 1$ (prob $(r-1)/r$)

$v_1 \sim D_1$. Adversary continues with prob $1 - \sigma(v_1)$.

Remaining $r-1$ rounds form a subgame. By inductive hypothesis, $\Pr[\text{hit in subgame}] \leq 1/(\alpha \cdot (r-1))$.

After combining both cases and simplifying, the total is $\leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r)$.

SIMULATOR - ~1.5 min

Point to the left column - Claim 4.

For malicious $P_1$: the simulator $\mathcal{S}_1$ first extracts the adversary's input $x'$ using the Phase 1 simulator. Then it picks its own switch point $i^*$.

Before $i^*$, the simulator does not know $P_2$'s real input $y$. So it picks a random $\hat{y}$ and computes shares using $f(x', \hat{y})$. These look like cover distribution samples. At round $i^*$, it sends $x'$ to the trusted party and receives the real output $z_1 = f^1(x', y)$. From that point on, all shares encode the real output.

The gap analysis: if the adversary aborts before $i^*$, the honest party $P_2$ gets the wrong output based on $\hat{y}$ instead of $y$. But by Lemma 2, this happens with probability at most $1/p$. If the adversary aborts after $i^*$, both parties have the correct output and the simulation is perfect.

Claim 5 for malicious $P_2$ is symmetric - same construction, same analysis.

With the protocol and simulator in place, let me state the main feasibility results.

Simulator Construction

Claim 4: Malicious $P_1$

Goal: Construct $\mathcal{S}_1$ in fair ideal world s.t. $\{\mathsf{IDEAL}\} \stackrel{1/p}{\approx} \{\mathsf{HYBRID}\}$
  1. Extract input: Run Phase 1 simulator, get $\mathcal{A}$'s input $x'$
  2. Pick $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$
  3. Rounds $i < i^*$: Don't know $y$. Pick $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$, compute $a_i = f^1(x', \hat{y})$. XOR-split and send to $\mathcal{A}$
  4. Round $i^*$: Send $x'$ to trusted party. Receive $z_1 = f^1(x', y)$
  5. Rounds $i \geq i^*$: Set $a_i = z_1$. Simulation is perfect

Gap analysis: If $\mathcal{A}$ aborts at $i^*$, honest $P_2$ gets $f^2(\hat{x}, y)$ (wrong) in hybrid but $f^2(x,y)$ (correct) in ideal. By Lemma 2: $\Pr \leq 1/p$.

Claim 5: Malicious $P_2$

Goal: Construct $\mathcal{S}_2$ in fair ideal world s.t. $\{\mathsf{IDEAL}\} \stackrel{1/p}{\approx} \{\mathsf{HYBRID}\}$
  1. Extract input: Get $\mathcal{A}$'s input $y'$
  2. Pick $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$
  3. Rounds $i < i^*$: Pick fresh $\hat{x} \leftarrow X$, compute $b_i = f^2(\hat{x}, y')$, XOR-split and send
  4. Round $i^*$: Send $y'$ to trusted party. Receive $z_2 = f^2(x, y')$
  5. Rounds $i \geq i^*$: Set $b_i = z_2$

Gap: If $\mathcal{A}$ aborts before $i^*$, $P_1$ gets $f^1(\hat{x}, y')$ (wrong). Same $1/p$ bound.

Privacy is immediate: $\mathcal{A}$'s view is identical in both worlds (same XOR shares, same MACs). The only difference is the honest party's output, bounded by $1/p + \mathrm{negl}(n)$.

THEOREM 3 - ~1 min

The first feasibility result: if at least one input domain has polynomial size, the protocol achieves $1/p$-security, privacy, and also security-with-abort simultaneously.

Why it works: the protocol runs $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$ rounds. Since $|Y_n|$ is poly, this is polynomial time. The cover distribution for $a_i$ is $f(x, \hat{y})$ for uniform $\hat{y}$, giving $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$. Lemma 2 gives abort probability $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = |Y_n|/(|Y_n| \cdot p) = 1/p$.

A key bonus: this protocol also achieves security-with-abort. The trick is to choose $i^*$ from $\{2,\ldots,r+1\}$ and set $b_{i^*-1} = \bot$. This lets malicious $P_2$ detect $i^*$, but that is fine since $P_1$ already has the output by then.

The next result handles the case of polynomial-size range.

Theorem 3: Polynomial-Size Domain (Section 3.2)

Theorem 3: If $|Y_n| = \mathrm{poly}(n)$ (or symmetrically $|X_n|$), then for every polynomial $p$, there exists an $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$-round protocol that $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$, privately computes $f$, and (with a small modification) securely-with-abort computes $f$, assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Proof sketch

  • Protocol: Use the gradual release construction with $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$ rounds and $\mathsf{ShareGen}_r$
  • Cover distribution: $a_i = f(x, \hat{y})$ for $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$. Then $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$
  • Security bound: By Lemma 2, $\Pr[\text{abort at } i^*] \leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r) = |Y_n|/(p \cdot |Y_n|) = 1/p$
  • $1/p$-security: Follows from Claims 4 (malicious $P_1$) and 5 (malicious $P_2$)
  • Privacy: Adversary's view is simulatable (XOR shares are uniformly distributed)
  • Security-with-abort: Modify by choosing $i^* \leftarrow \{2,\ldots,r+1\}$, set $b_{i^*-1} = \bot$

Round complexity

Upper bound: $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$ (Theorem 3)

Lower bound: $\Omega(\min\{p, |X_n|, |Y_n|\})$ (Section 5)

For binary output: $O(p)$ rounds. Gap remains for general case.

Assumption

Enhanced trapdoor permutations (imply OT, sufficient for general 2PC via Yao/GMW). No random oracles or non-standard assumptions.

THEOREM 6 - ~1 min

Now the harder case: when the domains are super-polynomial but at least one output range is polynomial-size. Here we cannot use the simple cover distribution from Theorem 3 because sampling $f(x, \hat{y})$ may not cover all possible outputs well enough.

The fix: use a mixed cover distribution. With probability $1/p$, sample $a_i$ uniformly from $Z^1_n$. With the remaining probability, sample $a_i = f^1(x, \hat{y})$ as before. This ensures every output value has probability at least $1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$, giving $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$.

The cost: the round complexity grows to $O(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|)$ - quadratic in $p$ instead of linear. Also, this protocol does NOT automatically achieve security-with-abort. The mixed distribution introduces a statistical gap of $O(1/p)$ in the simulation for malicious $P_2$.

Now let me show that these polynomial-size boundaries are tight.

Theorem 6: Polynomial-Size Range (Section 3.3)

Theorem 6: If $|Z^1_n| = \mathrm{poly}(n)$ (or symmetrically $|Z^2_n|$), then for every polynomial $p$, there exists an $O(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|)$-round protocol that $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$ and privately computes $f$, assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Key changes from Theorem 3

Modified cover distribution ($\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}$)

  • With prob $1/p$: $a_i \leftarrow \mathrm{Uniform}(Z^1_n)$
  • With prob $1 - 1/p$: $a_i = f^1(x, \hat{y})$ for $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$
  • Guarantees $\Pr[a_i = z] \geq \frac{1}{p \cdot |Z^1_n|}$ for all $z$
  • So $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$

Why no security-with-abort?

  • If malicious $P_2$ aborts before $i^*$: $P_1$ outputs $a_i$ from the mixed distribution
  • Real-world distribution of $a_i$ differs from ideal by $O(1/p)$
  • This gap is tolerable for $1/p$-security but prevents achieving the abort ideal
  • Theorems 9 and 11 show this is inherent

Round complexity

Rounds: $r = p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|$

Bound: $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = \frac{p \cdot |Z^1_n|}{p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|} = 1/p$

Security

Claim 7 (malicious $P_1$): same as Theorem 3
Claim 8 (malicious $P_2$): statistical gap $\leq 1/p$

THEOREM 9 - ~2 min

Important impossibility result. Walk through methodically.

The equality function on strings of super-polynomial length. Both input domains are super-polynomial. The theorem says: for $p \geq 4$, no protocol can simultaneously achieve $1/p$-security and security-with-abort for this function.

The proof uses three experiments. First, the random experiment: $x$ and $y$ are chosen independently, so equality is zero with overwhelming probability. Second, the equal experiment: set $y = x$, so equality is always one. Third, the mixed experiment: flip a coin $c$. If $c = 0$, use random inputs. If $c = 1$, use equal inputs.

The adversary corrupts $P_1$, runs the protocol honestly, and checks $a_i$ after each round. If $a_i = 1$, it aborts and guesses $c = 1$. If $a_i$ is never one, it guesses $c = 0$.

Point to the two boxes - Equations 2 and 3.

In the ideal world, the probability of the event "adversary guesses correctly AND $P_2$'s output is wrong" is at most one-half. But in the real world, it is at least three-quarters minus a negligible term. The gap is at least one-quarter, which rules out $1/p$-security for $p \geq 4$.

Theorem 11 is even stronger - it does not require security-with-abort at all.

Theorem 9: Impossibility for Equality (Section 4.1)

Theorem 9: Let $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$ be equality on strings of length $\ell(n) = \omega(\log n)$. For $p \geq 4$, no protocol can simultaneously $\frac{1}{p}$-securely and securely-with-abort compute $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$.

Proof: Three experiments

  1. Random experiment ($\Pr_{\mathrm{rand}}$): $x, y \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\ell(n)}$ independently. $\mathrm{EQ} = 0$ w.h.p.
  2. Equal experiment ($\Pr_{\mathrm{eq}}$): $x \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\ell(n)}$, set $y = x$. $\mathrm{EQ} = 1$ always.
  3. Mixed experiment: Flip coin $c$. If $c=0$: random. If $c=1$: equal.
Claim 10 (from security-with-abort): $\Pr_{\mathrm{rand}}[a_0 = 1 \lor \cdots \lor a_r = 1]$ is $\mathrm{negl}(n)$. Otherwise a random-round aborter would cause $P_1$ to output 1 in the real world with non-negl probability, contradicting the abort ideal where $\mathrm{EQ}=0$ w.h.p.

The adversary $\mathcal{A}$ (corrupting $P_1$)

Run $\Pi$ honestly, compute $a_i$ after each round. If $a_i = 1$: abort, output "guess $c=1$". If never: complete protocol, output "guess $c=0$".

Eq (2): Ideal world

$\Pr^{\mathrm{ideal}}_3[\mathrm{guess} \wedge \mathrm{out}_2 \neq 1] \leq \frac{1}{2}$

Eq (3): Real world

$\Pr^{\mathrm{real}}_3[\mathrm{guess} \wedge \mathrm{out}_2 \neq 1]$

$\geq \frac{1}{2}(1-\mathrm{negl}) + \frac{1}{2}\Pr_{\mathrm{eq}}[i^* \leq j^*]$

$\geq \frac{3}{4} - \mathrm{negl}(n)$

Gap $\geq 1/4$. Rules out $1/p$-security for $p \geq 4$.

THEOREM 11 - ~1.5 min

This is the strongest impossibility result. There exists a functionality that cannot be $1/p$-securely computed for any $p \geq 2$. Note: this is $1/p$-security alone, with no security-with-abort requirement.

The function is Swap with embedded information-theoretic MACs. Each party's input includes its value, a MAC key, and a MAC tag on the other party's value. The functionality outputs both values if all tags verify.

The adversary $P^*_1$ runs the protocol honestly until some round where the MAC verification passes on the received value $a_i$. Since the MAC is information-theoretically secure, a passing check means $a_i$ equals $x_2$ with overwhelming probability. At that point, $P_1$ aborts with the correct value.

Since one party must learn the other's value first with probability at least one-half, and in the fair ideal world this one-sided outcome is impossible, the gap is at least one-half. This rules out $p \geq 2$.

Let me now summarize the complete picture.

Theorem 11: Impossibility for Swap (Section 4.2)

Theorem 11: There exists a functionality that cannot be $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computed for $p \geq 2$.

The Swap function with embedded MACs

$P_1$ input: $(x_1,\; t_1,\; k_2,\; k'_2,\; t'_2)$   $P_2$ input: $(x_2,\; t_2,\; k_1,\; k'_1,\; t'_1)$

$$\mathrm{Swap}_n = \begin{cases} (x_1, x_2) & \text{if } \mathrm{Vrfy}_{k_1}(x_1, t_1) = \mathrm{Vrfy}_{k_2}(x_2, t_2) = 1 \\ \bot & \text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

$(k'_i, t'_i)$: extra MAC key/tag on $x_i$ under key $k'_i$. Ignored by functionality, used by adversary to verify.

Proof

  • Ideal world: $P_1$ cannot "win" (get $x_2$ while $P_2$ doesn't get $x_1$).
  • Adversary $P^*_1$: Run honestly until first round $i$ where $\mathrm{Vrfy}_{k'_2}(a_i, t'_2) = 1$. Then $a_i = x_2$ (by IT-MAC security). Output $a_i$ and abort.
  • Let $i$ = first round $P_1$ can verify $x_2$,   $j$ = first round $P_2$ can verify $x_1$.
  • $\Pr[i \leq j] + \Pr[j > i] = 1$   $\Rightarrow$   one of $P^*_1, P^*_2$ wins with $\Pr \geq 1/2 - \mathrm{negl}(n)$
  • Ideal-world win: $\mathrm{negl}(n)$.   Gap $\geq 1/2$. Rules out $p \geq 2$.
Why IT-MAC? The proof is statistical. A computationally-secure MAC allows forgery by powerful adversaries.

COMPLETE PICTURE - ~1 min

Walk through the table row by row.

Theorem 3: polynomial-size input domain gives all three guarantees - $1/p$-security, security-with-abort, and privacy. Theorem 6: polynomial-size output range gives $1/p$-security plus privacy. Theorem 9: super-polynomial input domains means we cannot combine $1/p$-security with security-with-abort, for $p \geq 4$. Theorem 11: if all domains and ranges are super-polynomial, no $1/p$-security at all, for $p \geq 2$.

The feasibility and impossibility results match exactly at the polynomial-size boundary. Partial fairness is possible if and only if at least one domain or range has polynomial size. This settles the open problem from Goldreich's textbook.

OPEN QUESTIONS - ~1 min

The paper identifies three open problems in Section 5.

First, fair exchange of digital signatures - Theorem 11 rules this out since exchanging signatures is essentially a Swap. So what is the right notion of partial fairness for that setting?

Second, the round complexity gap: the upper bound is $O(p^2 \cdot |Z|)$ and the lower bound is $\Omega(\min\{p, |X|, |Y|\})$. For binary output this is $O(p^2)$ versus $\Omega(p)$ - a quadratic gap. Whether the lower bound can be improved or a more efficient protocol exists remains open.

Third, multi-party partial fairness with dishonest majority - almost entirely open beyond coin tossing. The Lemma 2 approach is fundamentally two-party because the adversary's strategy in the multi-party case depends on all values seen by all corrupt parties, not just one.

Let me wrap up.

Open Questions (Section 5)

1. Fair exchange of signatures: Theorem 11 rules out $\frac{1}{p}$-security for Swap. Exchanging digital signatures is a form of Swap. What is the right notion of partial fairness for this setting?
2. Round complexity gap: Upper bound $O(p^2 \cdot |Z|)$ vs. lower bound $\Omega(\min\{p, |X|, |Y|\})$. For binary output: $O(p^2)$ vs. $\Omega(p)$. Can the lower bound be improved to $\Omega(p^2)$, or can a protocol achieve $O(p)$ rounds?
3. Multi-party with dishonest majority: Almost entirely open beyond coin tossing. General partial fairness with $t \geq n/2$ corruptions is unknown. The Lemma 2 approach is fundamentally two-party.

CLOSING - ~30 sec

To summarize: Gordon and Katz gave a clean, simulation-based definition of partial fairness - $1/p$-security - and showed it is achievable exactly when at least one domain or range has polynomial size. Their gradual release protocol achieves this bound, and the impossibility results for the equality and swap functions show the polynomial-size condition is tight. This settles Goldreich's open problem on defining partial fairness within the real/ideal paradigm.
