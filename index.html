OPENING - ~1 min

Good afternoon everyone. So today I'll be presenting this paper by Gordon and Katz on partial fairness in secure two-party computation. It was published at Eurocrypt 2010.

Quick context: back in 1986, Cleve proved that we can't have complete fairness in general two-party computation. After that, the whole field just settled for security-with-abort, which gives us privacy and correctness but basically zero fairness.

BACKGROUND - ~1.5 min

Okay so this is the standard two-party computation setup, goes back to Yao in '86. We have two parties, $P_1$ with input $x$ from some domain $X_n$, and $P_2$ with input $y$ from $Y_n$. They want to jointly compute a function $f$ that maps their inputs to outputs $Z^1_n \times Z^2_n$. Here $n$ is the security parameter, it controls everything.

Security is defined using the real-ideal paradigm. We can see the two columns here.

Point to the left column.

On the left is the real world: no trusted party, the parties just run a protocol $\Pi$. The adversary controls one party, can send whatever it wants, can abort whenever. The output we care about is denoted $\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)$.

Point to the right column.

On the right is the ideal world: there's a trusted party that just receives the inputs, computes $f(x,y)$, and hands out the outputs. The adversary here is the simulator $\mathcal{S}$, and it can only talk to the trusted party. Output is $\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)$.

A protocol is secure if for every real-world adversary, we can find a simulator such that these two distributions look the same to any efficient distinguisher.

Point to the table.

Now there are four properties we want. Privacy, correctness, input independence - all achievable since Yao. But fairness, meaning both get output or neither does, that's the problem. Cleve showed in '86 that it's impossible. Let me show you why.

Secure Two-Party Computation

Two parties $P_1$ (input $x \in X_n$) and $P_2$ (input $y \in Y_n$) jointly compute $f_n : X_n \times Y_n \to Z^1_n \times Z^2_n$, where $n$ is the security parameter. Security is defined via the real/ideal paradigm.

Real world

No trusted party. Parties execute protocol $\Pi$. Adversary $\mathcal{A}$ controls one party, sends arbitrary messages, can abort at any time.

Output: $\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)$ = joint output of $\mathcal{A}$ and the honest party.

Ideal world

A trusted party receives inputs, computes $f(x,y)$, delivers outputs. Adversary $\mathcal{S}$ (simulator) can only interact with the trusted party.

Output: $\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)$ = joint output of $\mathcal{S}$ and the honest party.

Security properties

  • Privacy: adversary's view can be simulated from output alone (achievable, Yao 86)
  • Correctness: honest party always gets $f^i(x,y)$ (achievable)
  • Input independence: adversary commits input before seeing honest party's (achievable)
  • Fairness: both get output, or neither does (impossible, Cleve 86)

CLEVE'S IMPOSSIBILITY - ~1.5 min

So this is Cleve's 1986 result, and it's really the starting point for everything in this paper.

What Cleve showed is: take any $r$-round coin-flipping protocol. A malicious party can always bias the outcome by $\Omega(1/r)$. So complete fairness is just impossible for general 2PC.

The intuition is pretty clean. Let $a_i$ be $P_1$'s output if $P_2$ aborts after round $i$, and similarly $b_i$ for the other direction. At round zero, nobody knows the answer. At round $r$, both know it. So somewhere in between, one party learns the answer before the other. That party can just abort right there and walk away with the output while the other party gets nothing. Cleve showed the advantage from this is $\Omega(1/r)$. And by the way, this bound is tight - Moran, Naor, and Segev showed $O(1/r)$ is achievable for coin tossing in 2009.

Point to the bottom grid.

So what did the field do after Cleve? They went with security-with-abort. The idea is: the trusted party computes $f(x,y)$, sends $z_1$ to $P_1$ first, and then $P_1$ can either say "continue" or "abort". If it aborts, $P_2$ just gets $\bot$. Gets nothing.

This keeps privacy, correctness, input independence - all fine. But fairness? Zero. $P_1$ can always learn the output and block $P_2$. Goldreich actually flagged in his textbook, Section 7.7.1.1, that properly defining partial fairness was an open problem. And that's exactly what Gordon and Katz solve.

Cleve's Impossibility (1986)

Theorem [Cleve 86]: For any $r$-round two-party coin-flipping protocol, a malicious adversary can bias the outcome by $\Omega(1/r)$. Complete fairness is impossible for general 2PC without honest majority.

The argument (intuition)

  • Let $a_i$ = $P_1$'s output if $P_2$ aborts after round $i$;   $b_i$ = $P_2$'s output if $P_1$ aborts after round $i$
  • At round 0: nobody knows $f(x,y)$.   At round $r$: both know $f(x,y)$
  • There exists a threshold round where one party's output transitions from "unknown" to "known" before the other's
  • That party can abort at the threshold and walk away with the answer
  • Advantage: $\Omega(1/r)$ (tight: Moran-Naor-Segev 2009 showed $O(1/r)$ is achievable for coin tossing)

Consequence: Security-with-abort

How it works

  • Trusted party computes $f(x,y) = (z_1, z_2)$
  • Sends $z_1$ to $P_1$ first
  • $P_1$ then sends continue or abort
  • If abort: trusted party sends $\bot$ to $P_2$

Properties

  • Privacy: preserved (negligible gap)
  • Correctness: preserved
  • Input independence: preserved
  • Fairness: none

Goldreich (Section 7.7.1.1): defining partial fairness was an open problem.

THE DEFINITION - ~2 min

So this is the paper's main idea, Definition 1 in Section 2. Let me break it down.

Read the definition box.

A protocol $1/p$-securely computes $f$ if for every PPT adversary, there exists a simulator in the complete fairness ideal world such that the absolute difference between the distinguisher's probability on REAL versus IDEAL is at most $1/p(n)$ plus negligible. Here $p$ is any polynomial in the security parameter $n$, so the gap can be made as small as we want, like $1/n^{100}$, just not negligible.

The crucial thing is: the ideal world here is the same fair ideal world we'd use for complete security. Both parties always get the correct output. The only thing they relax is the distinguishing gap.

Point to the comparison table.

These three rows compare the notions. Full security: fair ideal world, negligible gap - but Cleve says it's impossible. Security-with-abort: weakened ideal world where the adversary can block, negligible gap - achievable, but we get zero fairness. $1/p$-security: fair ideal world, but $1/p$ gap. That's the sweet spot. We're comparing to the best possible world, just allowing a small slack.

One subtlety worth knowing: Katz had proposed something called $1/p$-security-with-abort in his STOC 2007 paper. But that compares to the abort ideal, so it actually gives no fairness at all despite the name. The paper mentions this in footnote 1.

Alright, so to actually build a protocol that achieves this, we need something called a cover distribution.

The Definition: $\frac{1}{p}$-Security (Definition 1)

Definition 1: Protocol $\Pi$ $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$ if for every polynomial $p$ and every PPT adversary $\mathcal{A}$, there exists a PPT simulator $\mathcal{S}$ in the complete fairness ideal world such that for all PPT distinguishers $D$:
$$\left|\Pr[D(\mathsf{REAL}_{\Pi,\mathcal{A}(z)}(x,y,n)) = 1] - \Pr[D(\mathsf{IDEAL}_{f,\mathcal{S}(z)}(x,y,n)) = 1]\right| \leq \frac{1}{p(n)} + \mathrm{negl}(n)$$

Three notions compared

Notion Ideal world Gap Fairness?
Full security Fair (both get output) negligible Complete, but impossible
Security-with-abort Abort ($P_1$ can block $P_2$) negligible None
Definition 1 ($1/p$-security) Fair (both get output) $1/p(n)$ Partial
The ideal world is the same as for full security. The only relaxation is the gap. Since we compare to the fair ideal, the protocol is "almost as good" as a trusted party.
Contrast with Katz [STOC 07]: "$\frac{1}{p}$-security-with-abort" compares to the abort ideal. That gives no fairness despite the name (footnote 1 in the paper).

COVER DISTRIBUTIONS - ~1 min

Read the definition box.

Before I describe the protocol, I need to introduce cover distributions. $D_1$ covers $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$ if for every $z$, the probability of $z$ under $D_1$ is at least $\alpha$ times its probability under $D_2$. Formally, $\Pr_{a \leftarrow D_1}[a = z] \geq \alpha \cdot \Pr_{a \leftarrow D_2}[a = z]$.

And the intuition: $D_2$ is the real output distribution, what $f(x,y)$ actually gives. $D_1$ is the fake distribution we use before the switch. Every value that's possible under $D_2$ must also be reasonably likely under $D_1$. Otherwise, if the adversary sees something that can't come from $D_1$, it knows the switch happened.

Point to the two columns.

On the left, Choice 1 is just uniform over $Z$. Since $D_1(z) = 1/|Z|$ and $D_2(z) \leq 1$, we get $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$. This gets used in Theorem 6. On the right, Choice 2 is a mix: with probability $1/p$ sample uniform, otherwise sample from $D_2$ directly. Same $\alpha$, but $D_1$ ends up much closer to $D_2$, which is better for the Theorem 3 construction.

Point to the formula at the bottom.

And here's why $\alpha$ matters so much. Lemma 2 will give us a bound of $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$ on the adversary's advantage. So if we plug in $\alpha = 1/(p|Z|)$ and $r = p^2|Z|$, we get exactly $1/p$. That's where the security bound comes from.

Okay, so with cover distributions in hand, let me describe the actual protocol.

Cover Distributions

Distribution $D_1$ (fake/cover) over output space $Z$ covers $D_2$ (real output distribution) with parameter $\alpha$ if for every $z \in Z$: $$\Pr_{a \leftarrow D_1}[a = z] \geq \alpha \cdot \Pr_{a \leftarrow D_2}[a = z]$$

$D_2$ is the real output distribution (i.e. $f(x,y)$), and $D_1$ is the fake distribution used before the switch. Every value possible under $D_2$ must be reasonably likely under $D_1$, otherwise the adversary detects the switch.

Choice 1: Uniform distribution

  • $D_1 = \mathrm{Uniform}(Z)$
  • $D_1(z) = 1/|Z|$, and $D_2(z) \leq 1$
  • So $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$ works
  • Used in Theorem 6 (poly-size range)

Choice 2: Mixed distribution

  • With prob $1/p$: sample $\mathrm{Uniform}(Z)$
  • With prob $1 - 1/p$: sample from $D_2$ itself
  • Same $\alpha$, but $D_1$ is much closer to $D_2$
  • Used in Theorem 3 (poly-size domain)

Why $\alpha$ matters

Lemma 2 will show that the adversary's probability of detecting the switch point is $\leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r)$. With $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z|)$ and $r = p^2 \cdot |Z|$:

$$\frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r} = \frac{p \cdot |Z|}{p^2 \cdot |Z|} = \frac{1}{p}$$

THE PROTOCOL - ~2 min

Point to the left column.

So the protocol has two phases. Phase 1 is the setup. The parties run a secure-with-abort protocol to compute this helper functionality called ShareGen-prime.

What does ShareGen do? It picks a secret switch point $i^*$ uniformly from 1 to $r$. For rounds before $i^*$, it samples fake values from the cover distribution $D_1$. From $i^*$ onward, it uses the real output $f(x,y)$. Then it XOR-splits each value into two halves - $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$ - generates MAC keys and tags for authentication, and gives $P_1$ the first halves and $P_2$ the second halves, along with the MAC info.

Point to the right column.

Phase 2 is where the actual exchange happens - gradual release. Each round, $P_2$ goes first: sends its half with a MAC tag. $P_1$ checks the MAC, and if it's good, XORs the two halves to get $a_i$. Then $P_1$ sends its half to $P_2$, same deal. At the end, each party just outputs the last value they successfully verified.

Now there's an important asymmetry. $P_2$ sends first every round, so $P_1$ always has a one-round lead. That's why the proof handles corrupt $P_1$ and corrupt $P_2$ separately: Claims 4 and 5 for Theorem 3, Claims 7 and 8 for Theorem 6.

And the MACs are there to stop the adversary from sending garbage share halves. If the MAC doesn't check out, we just ignore it.

Now, the security of all of this rests on one key lemma.

The Protocol (Section 3)

Phase 1: Setup via $\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}$

Run any secure-with-abort protocol for $\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}(x,y)$.

  1. Pick switch point $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$ uniformly
  2. For $i < i^*$: sample $a_i \leftarrow D_1$, $b_i \leftarrow D'_1$ (cover distribution)
  3. For $i \geq i^*$: set $a_i = f^1(x,y)$, $b_i = f^2(x,y)$ (real output)
  4. XOR-split each value: $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$
  5. Generate MAC keys $k_i$ and tags $t_i = \mathrm{Mac}_{k_i}(a_i^{(j)})$
  6. Give $P_1$ its halves $\{a_i^{(1)}, b_i^{(1)}\}$ + MAC tags/keys
  7. Give $P_2$ its halves $\{a_i^{(2)}, b_i^{(2)}\}$ + MAC tags/keys

Phase 2: Gradual release

For each round $i = 1, \ldots, r$:

  1. $P_2$ sends $(a_i^{(2)},\;\mathrm{tag})$ to $P_1$
  2. $P_1$ verifies MAC. If valid: $a_i = a_i^{(1)} \oplus a_i^{(2)}$
  3. $P_1$ sends $(b_i^{(1)},\;\mathrm{tag})$ to $P_2$
  4. $P_2$ verifies MAC. If valid: $b_i = b_i^{(1)} \oplus b_i^{(2)}$

Each party outputs the last successfully verified value.

Asymmetry

$P_2$ sends first each round, so $P_1$ has a one-round information lead. The proof handles malicious $P_1$ (Claim 4) and malicious $P_2$ (Claim 5) separately.

Why MACs?

Prevent the adversary from sending modified share halves. If a MAC check fails, the received message is simply ignored.

LEMMA 2 - ~2 min

Most technical slide. Don't rush.

Read the Lemma 2 statement and formula box.

This is really the heart of the paper. Lemma 2 says: let $D_1$ cover $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$, and let the switch point $i^*$ be uniform in 1 to $r$. The adversary sees values from $D_1$ before the switch and from $D_2$ after. Then the probability the adversary aborts at exactly the switch point is at most $1/(\alpha \cdot r)$. No matter what adaptive strategy it uses.

The proof goes by induction on $r$. Base case $r = 1$ is simple. For the inductive step, after seeing $v_1$, the adversary's strategy is just some function $\sigma$ mapping values to abort probabilities.

Point to the left column.

If $i^* = 1$, the adversary sees a value from $D_2$ and aborts with probability $\sigma(v_1)$. So the hit probability is $(1/r) \sum_z D_2(z) \sigma(z)$. But here's the thing - the cover property constrains this. Since $D_1(z) \geq \alpha \cdot D_2(z)$, the adversary can't just concentrate all its aborting on values that are more likely under $D_2$.

Point to the right column.

If $i^*$ is bigger than 1, then $v_1$ comes from $D_1$, and the remaining $r-1$ rounds are just a subgame that we handle by the inductive hypothesis.

Alright, so now let me show how the simulator uses this.

Lemma 2: Bounding the Abort Probability

Lemma 2: Let $D_1$ cover $D_2$ with parameter $\alpha$, and let $i^*$ be uniform in $\{1,\ldots,r\}$. The adversary observes $v_i \sim D_1$ for $i < i^*$ and $v_i \sim D_2$ for $i \geq i^*$. For any adaptive abort strategy:
$$\Pr[\text{adversary aborts at exactly } i^*] \leq \frac{1}{\alpha \cdot r}$$

Proof (induction on $r$)

  • Base case ($r=1$): $i^* = 1$ always. $\Pr[\text{abort at } i^*] \leq 1 \leq 1/\alpha = 1/(\alpha \cdot 1)$ since $\alpha \leq 1$.
  • Inductive step: After observing $v_1$, the adversary's strategy is $\sigma: Z \to [0,1]$ (probability of aborting).

Case: $i^* = 1$ (prob $1/r$)

$v_1 \sim D_2$. Adversary aborts with prob $\sigma(v_1)$.

$\Pr[\text{hit}] = \frac{1}{r}\sum_z D_2(z) \cdot \sigma(z)$

Cover property constrains this: $D_1(z) \geq \alpha \cdot D_2(z)$, so $\sigma$ cannot concentrate on values more likely under $D_2$.

Case: $i^* > 1$ (prob $(r-1)/r$)

$v_1 \sim D_1$. Adversary continues with prob $1 - \sigma(v_1)$.

Remaining $r-1$ rounds form a subgame. By inductive hypothesis, $\Pr[\text{hit in subgame}] \leq 1/(\alpha \cdot (r-1))$.

After combining both cases and simplifying, the total is $\leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r)$.

SIMULATOR - ~1.5 min

Point to the left column.

Read the goal box for Claim 4.

Okay so here's the simulator for corrupt $P_1$, that's Claim 4. The goal is to construct a simulator $\mathcal{S}_1$ in the fair ideal world so that IDEAL and HYBRID are $1/p$-close. Here's what it does: first, extract the adversary's input $x'$ from Phase 1. Then pick a switch point $i^*$. Before $i^*$, the simulator doesn't know $P_2$'s real input $y$, so it just picks a random $\hat{y}$, computes $f^1(x', \hat{y})$, XOR-splits it, sends it to the adversary. At round $i^*$, it sends $x'$ to the trusted party and gets back the real output $z_1 = f^1(x', y)$. From then on, everything is real and the simulation is perfect.

Where does the gap come from? If the adversary aborts before $i^*$, $P_2$ gets output based on a random $\hat{y}$ instead of the real $y$, which is wrong. But by Lemma 2, the chance of aborting at exactly $i^*$ is at most $1/p$. And if the adversary aborts after $i^*$, both parties already have the correct output, so there's no gap at all.

Point to the right column.

Claim 5 for corrupt $P_2$ is basically symmetric. Extract $y'$, pick $i^*$, use random $\hat{x}$ to compute $f^2(\hat{x}, y')$ before the switch, then send $y'$ to the trusted party and use the real output from $i^*$ on. Same $1/p$ bound.

Point to the box at the bottom.

Privacy is actually free here. The adversary's view is identical in both worlds because XOR shares are uniform and the MACs are information-theoretic. The only difference is the honest party's output, and that's bounded by $1/p$ plus negligible.

So with the protocol and simulator done, let me state the main feasibility results.

Simulator Construction

Claim 4: Malicious $P_1$

Goal: Construct $\mathcal{S}_1$ in fair ideal world s.t. $\{\mathsf{IDEAL}\} \stackrel{1/p}{\approx} \{\mathsf{HYBRID}\}$
  1. Extract input: Run Phase 1 simulator, get $\mathcal{A}$'s input $x'$
  2. Pick $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$
  3. Rounds $i < i^*$: Don't know $y$. Pick $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$, compute $a_i = f^1(x', \hat{y})$. XOR-split and send to $\mathcal{A}$
  4. Round $i^*$: Send $x'$ to trusted party. Receive $z_1 = f^1(x', y)$
  5. Rounds $i \geq i^*$: Set $a_i = z_1$. Simulation is perfect

Gap: If $\mathcal{A}$ aborts at $i^*$, honest $P_2$ gets $f^2(\hat{x}, y)$ (wrong) in hybrid but $f^2(x,y)$ (correct) in ideal. By Lemma 2: $\Pr \leq 1/p$.

Claim 5: Malicious $P_2$

Goal: Construct $\mathcal{S}_2$ in fair ideal world s.t. $\{\mathsf{IDEAL}\} \stackrel{1/p}{\approx} \{\mathsf{HYBRID}\}$
  1. Extract input: Get $\mathcal{A}$'s input $y'$
  2. Pick $i^* \leftarrow \{1,\ldots,r\}$
  3. Rounds $i < i^*$: Pick fresh $\hat{x} \leftarrow X$, compute $b_i = f^2(\hat{x}, y')$, XOR-split and send
  4. Round $i^*$: Send $y'$ to trusted party. Receive $z_2 = f^2(x, y')$
  5. Rounds $i \geq i^*$: Set $b_i = z_2$

Gap: If $\mathcal{A}$ aborts before $i^*$, $P_1$ gets $f^1(\hat{x}, y')$ (wrong). Same $1/p$ bound.

Privacy is immediate: $\mathcal{A}$'s view is identical in both worlds (same XOR shares, same MACs). The only difference is the honest party's output, bounded by $1/p + \mathrm{negl}(n)$.

THEOREM 3 - ~1 min

Read the theorem statement.

Alright, first feasibility result. If at least one input domain is polynomial-size, meaning $|Y_n|$, that's the number of possible inputs for $P_2$, grows polynomially in $n$, then for every polynomial $p$ there's an $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$-round protocol that $1/p$-securely computes $f$, privately computes $f$, and with a small modification securely-with-abort computes $f$. All three properties. Assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

The proof sketch is listed below. We use the gradual release construction with $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$ rounds. The cover distribution is just $a_i = f(x, \hat{y})$ for a random $\hat{y}$, which gives $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$. Lemma 2 then gives us abort probability $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = |Y_n|/(p \cdot |Y_n|) = 1/p$. Security follows from Claims 4 and 5. Privacy is free because the XOR shares are uniform.

And here's a nice bonus: this protocol also gets us security-with-abort. The trick is small - we choose $i^*$ from $\{2, \ldots, r+1\}$ instead of $\{1, \ldots, r\}$, and set $b_{i^*-1} = \bot$. This lets corrupt $P_2$ detect $i^*$, but that's fine because $P_1$ already has the output by then.

Point to the bottom grid.

On round complexity: upper bound is $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$, lower bound from Section 5 is $\Omega(\min\{p, |X_n|, |Y_n|\})$. For binary output that's just $O(p)$ rounds. The assumption - enhanced trapdoor permutations - implies OT, which is enough for general 2PC via Yao or GMW. No random oracles, nothing exotic.

Now, what about when only the output range is polynomial-size?

Theorem 3: Polynomial-Size Domain (Section 3.2)

Theorem 3: If $|Y_n| = \mathrm{poly}(n)$ (or symmetrically $|X_n|$), then for every polynomial $p$, there exists an $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$-round protocol that $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$, privately computes $f$, and (with a small modification) securely-with-abort computes $f$, assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Proof sketch

  • Protocol: Use the gradual release construction with $r = p \cdot |Y_n|$ rounds and $\mathsf{ShareGen}_r$
  • Cover distribution: $a_i = f(x, \hat{y})$ for $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$. Then $\alpha = 1/|Y_n|$
  • Security bound: By Lemma 2, $\Pr[\text{abort at } i^*] \leq 1/(\alpha \cdot r) = |Y_n|/(p \cdot |Y_n|) = 1/p$
  • $1/p$-security follows from Claims 4 (malicious $P_1$) and 5 (malicious $P_2$)
  • Privacy: Adversary's view is simulatable (XOR shares are uniformly distributed)
  • Security-with-abort: Modify by choosing $i^* \leftarrow \{2,\ldots,r+1\}$, set $b_{i^*-1} = \bot$

Round complexity

Upper bound: $O(p \cdot |Y_n|)$ (Theorem 3)

Lower bound: $\Omega(\min\{p, |X_n|, |Y_n|\})$ (Section 5)

For binary output: $O(p)$ rounds. Gap remains for general case.

Assumption

Enhanced trapdoor permutations (imply OT, sufficient for general 2PC via Yao/GMW). No random oracles or non-standard assumptions.

THEOREM 6 - ~1.5 min

Read the theorem statement.

Now the harder case. If at least one output range $|Z^1_n|$ is polynomial, that's the number of possible output values for $P_1$, we get an $O(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|)$-round protocol for $1/p$-security and privacy. Same assumption. But this time we don't get security-with-abort.

Point to the left column.

The big change is the cover distribution. We use a mix: with probability $1/p$, sample $a_i$ uniformly from $Z^1_n$; with probability $1 - 1/p$, sample $a_i = f^1(x, \hat{y})$ using a random $\hat{y}$. This guarantees every output value has probability at least $1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$, so $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$.

Point to the right column.

But here's the catch - this protocol doesn't give us security-with-abort. Why? If corrupt $P_2$ aborts before $i^*$, $P_1$ outputs some $a_i$ from this mixed distribution. The real-world distribution of that output differs from the ideal by $O(1/p)$. That's fine for $1/p$-security, but the abort ideal needs a negligible gap, and we can't get that. And Theorems 9 and 11 show this isn't just a weakness of the proof - it's inherent.

Point to the bottom grid.

Round count is $r = p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|$. Lemma 2 gives $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = (p \cdot |Z^1_n|)/(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|) = 1/p$. Security goes through Claim 7 for corrupt $P_1$ (same as Theorem 3) and Claim 8 for corrupt $P_2$ (statistical gap at most $1/p$).

Now let me show that these polynomial-size boundaries are actually tight.

Theorem 6: Polynomial-Size Range (Section 3.3)

Theorem 6: If $|Z^1_n| = \mathrm{poly}(n)$ (or symmetrically $|Z^2_n|$), then for every polynomial $p$, there exists an $O(p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|)$-round protocol that $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computes $f$ and privately computes $f$, assuming enhanced trapdoor permutations.

Key changes from Theorem 3

Modified cover distribution ($\mathsf{ShareGen}'_{p,r}$)

  • With prob $1/p$: $a_i \leftarrow \mathrm{Uniform}(Z^1_n)$
  • With prob $1 - 1/p$: $a_i = f^1(x, \hat{y})$ for $\hat{y} \leftarrow Y_n$
  • Guarantees $\Pr[a_i = z] \geq \frac{1}{p \cdot |Z^1_n|}$ for all $z$
  • So $\alpha = 1/(p \cdot |Z^1_n|)$

Why no security-with-abort?

  • If malicious $P_2$ aborts before $i^*$: $P_1$ outputs $a_i$ from the mixed distribution
  • Real-world distribution of $a_i$ differs from ideal by $O(1/p)$
  • This gap is tolerable for $1/p$-security but prevents achieving the abort ideal
  • Theorems 9 and 11 show this is inherent

Round complexity

Rounds: $r = p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|$

Bound: $1/(\alpha \cdot r) = \frac{p \cdot |Z^1_n|}{p^2 \cdot |Z^1_n|} = 1/p$

Security

Claim 7 (malicious $P_1$): same as Theorem 3
Claim 8 (malicious $P_2$): statistical gap $\leq 1/p$

THEOREM 9 - ~2 min

Take this one slow, it's the key impossibility result.

So Theorem 9 is about the equality function $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$ on strings of length $\ell(n) = \omega(\log n)$, meaning both input domains are super-polynomial. What it says is: for $p \geq 4$, we can't have both $1/p$-security and security-with-abort at the same time.

The proof sets up three experiments. Random experiment: pick $x$ and $y$ independently, so equality is 0 with overwhelming probability. Equal experiment: pick $x$ uniformly and set $y = x$, so equality is always 1. Mixed experiment: flip a coin $c$ - heads we do random, tails we do equal.

Point to the Claim 10 box.

Then there's Claim 10. It uses the security-with-abort assumption to say that in the random experiment, the probability any intermediate $a_i$ equals 1 is negligible. Makes sense - if it weren't, an adversary could just abort at a random round and get $P_1$ to output 1 when the real answer is 0, which contradicts the abort ideal.

The adversary is simple: corrupt $P_1$, run honestly, check $a_i$ each round. If it ever sees $a_i = 1$, abort and guess $c = 1$. If it never sees it, finish the protocol and guess $c = 0$.

Point to the ideal world and real world boxes.

In the ideal world, the probability that the adversary guesses correctly AND $P_2$ doesn't output 1 is at most $1/2$, because the simulator has to commit before seeing the coin. But in the real world, that same probability is at least $1/2$ times $(1 - \mathrm{negl})$ plus $1/2$ times the probability that the switch happens before $P_2$ learns the answer, which works out to at least $3/4$ minus negligible. So the gap is at least $3/4 - 1/2 = 1/4$. That rules out $1/p$-security for $p \geq 4$.

Theorem 11 goes even further - it doesn't even need security-with-abort.

Theorem 9: Impossibility for Equality (Section 4.1)

Theorem 9: Let $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$ be equality on strings of length $\ell(n) = \omega(\log n)$. For $p \geq 4$, no protocol can simultaneously $\frac{1}{p}$-securely and securely-with-abort compute $\mathrm{EQ}_\ell$.

Proof: Three experiments

  1. Random experiment ($\Pr_{\mathrm{rand}}$): $x, y \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\ell(n)}$ independently. $\mathrm{EQ} = 0$ w.h.p.
  2. Equal experiment ($\Pr_{\mathrm{eq}}$): $x \leftarrow \{0,1\}^{\ell(n)}$, set $y = x$. $\mathrm{EQ} = 1$ always.
  3. Mixed experiment: Flip coin $c$. If $c=0$: random. If $c=1$: equal.
Claim 10 (from security-with-abort): $\Pr_{\mathrm{rand}}[a_0 = 1 \lor \cdots \lor a_r = 1]$ is $\mathrm{negl}(n)$. Otherwise a random-round aborter would cause $P_1$ to output 1 in the real world with non-negl probability, contradicting the abort ideal where $\mathrm{EQ}=0$ w.h.p.

The adversary $\mathcal{A}$ (corrupting $P_1$)

Run $\Pi$ honestly, compute $a_i$ after each round. If $a_i = 1$: abort, output "guess $c=1$". If never: complete protocol, output "guess $c=0$".

Eq (2): Ideal world

$\Pr^{\mathrm{ideal}}_3[\mathrm{guess} \wedge \mathrm{out}_2 \neq 1] \leq \frac{1}{2}$

Eq (3): Real world

$\Pr^{\mathrm{real}}_3[\mathrm{guess} \wedge \mathrm{out}_2 \neq 1]$

$\geq \frac{1}{2}(1-\mathrm{negl}) + \frac{1}{2}\Pr_{\mathrm{eq}}[i^* \leq j^*]$

$\geq \frac{3}{4} - \mathrm{negl}(n)$

Gap $\geq 1/4$. Rules out $1/p$-security for $p \geq 4$.

THEOREM 11 - ~1.5 min

This is the strongest result in the paper. There exists a function that just can't be $1/p$-securely computed for any $p \geq 2$. And note - this doesn't need security-with-abort. Just $1/p$-security alone is impossible.

The function is Swap with embedded information-theoretic MACs. $P_1$ has its value $x_1$, a tag $t_1$ for authentication, a key $k_2$ to verify the other party's value, and an extra key-tag pair $(k'_2, t'_2)$. The functionality outputs $(x_1, x_2)$ if both MACs verify, $\bot$ otherwise.

The clever part is those extra key-tag pairs. The functionality ignores them, but the adversary can use them to check during the protocol whether the value it's receiving is actually correct.

Here's the proof. In the ideal world, $P_1$ can't "win" - can't get $x_2$ while blocking $P_2$ from getting $x_1$, because the trusted party delivers both at once. But the adversary $P^*_1$ runs honestly until some round where $\mathrm{Vrfy}_{k'_2}(a_i, t'_2) = 1$. Since it's an IT-MAC, a passing check means $a_i = x_2$ with overwhelming probability. So $P_1$ grabs that and aborts.

Now let $i$ be the first round $P_1$ can verify, $j$ the first round $P_2$ can verify. One of them goes first, right? So $\Pr[i \leq j] + \Pr[j < i] = 1$, which means at least one of $P^*_1$ or $P^*_2$ wins with probability at least $1/2$. But in the ideal world that probability is negligible. Gap is at least $1/2$. Rules out $p \geq 2$.

And the box at the bottom explains why we need IT-MACs specifically - with computational MACs, a powerful adversary could forge, and the whole argument falls apart.

Let me pull everything together now.

Theorem 11: Impossibility for Swap (Section 4.2)

Theorem 11: There exists a functionality that cannot be $\frac{1}{p}$-securely computed for $p \geq 2$.

The Swap function with embedded MACs

$P_1$ input: $(x_1,\; t_1,\; k_2,\; k'_2,\; t'_2)$   $P_2$ input: $(x_2,\; t_2,\; k_1,\; k'_1,\; t'_1)$

$$\mathrm{Swap}_n = \begin{cases} (x_1, x_2) & \text{if } \mathrm{Vrfy}_{k_1}(x_1, t_1) = \mathrm{Vrfy}_{k_2}(x_2, t_2) = 1 \\ \bot & \text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

$(k'_i, t'_i)$: extra MAC key/tag on $x_i$ under key $k'_i$. Ignored by functionality, used by adversary to verify.

Proof

  • Ideal world: $P_1$ cannot "win" (get $x_2$ while $P_2$ doesn't get $x_1$).
  • Adversary $P^*_1$: Run honestly until first round $i$ where $\mathrm{Vrfy}_{k'_2}(a_i, t'_2) = 1$. Then $a_i = x_2$ (by IT-MAC security). Output $a_i$ and abort.
  • Let $i$ = first round $P_1$ can verify $x_2$,   $j$ = first round $P_2$ can verify $x_1$.
  • $\Pr[i \leq j] + \Pr[j > i] = 1$   $\Rightarrow$   one of $P^*_1, P^*_2$ wins with $\Pr \geq 1/2 - \mathrm{negl}(n)$
  • Ideal-world win: $\mathrm{negl}(n)$.   Gap $\geq 1/2$. Rules out $p \geq 2$.
Why IT-MAC? The proof is statistical. A computationally-secure MAC allows forgery by powerful adversaries.

COMPLETE PICTURE - ~1 min

Walk through the table row by row.

Okay so let me put it all together. Theorem 3: if an input domain is poly-size, we get everything - $1/p$-security, security-with-abort, privacy. Theorem 6: if an output range is poly-size, we get $1/p$-security and privacy, but not security-with-abort. Theorem 9: if both input domains are super-poly, like equality, we can't combine $1/p$-security with security-with-abort for $p \geq 4$. And Theorem 11: if everything is super-poly, like Swap, we can't even get $1/p$-security for $p \geq 2$.

So the feasibility and impossibility results line up perfectly at the polynomial-size boundary. Partial fairness is achievable if and only if at least one domain or range is polynomial. That's a tight characterization, and it settles the open problem from Goldreich, Section 7.7.1.1.

Point to the key dependencies below.

Few things to note: both feasibility results need enhanced trapdoor permutations. Theorem 9 needs the protocol to satisfy both $1/p$-security and security-with-abort simultaneously. Theorem 11 only needs $1/p$-security, which makes it a stronger impossibility.

OPEN QUESTIONS - ~1 min

The paper flags three open problems in Section 5.

First, fair exchange of digital signatures. Theorem 11 rules this out because exchanging signatures is basically a Swap. So the question is: what's the right notion of partial fairness for that setting?

Second, the round complexity gap. The upper bound is $O(p^2 \cdot |Z|)$ and the lower bound is $\Omega(\min\{p, |X|, |Y|\})$. For binary output that's $O(p^2)$ versus $\Omega(p)$ - a quadratic gap. Can we close it from either side?

Third, multi-party partial fairness with dishonest majority. This is almost completely open beyond coin tossing. The Lemma 2 technique is fundamentally two-party because in the multi-party case, the adversary's strategy depends on values seen by all corrupt parties, not just one.

Alright, let me wrap up.

Open Questions (Section 5)

1. Fair exchange of signatures: Theorem 11 rules out $\frac{1}{p}$-security for Swap. Exchanging digital signatures is a form of Swap. What is the right notion of partial fairness for this setting?
2. Round complexity gap: Upper bound $O(p^2 \cdot |Z|)$ vs. lower bound $\Omega(\min\{p, |X|, |Y|\})$. For binary output: $O(p^2)$ vs. $\Omega(p)$. Can the lower bound be improved to $\Omega(p^2)$, or can a protocol achieve $O(p)$ rounds?
3. Multi-party with dishonest majority: Almost entirely open beyond coin tossing. General partial fairness with $t \geq n/2$ corruptions is unknown. The Lemma 2 approach is fundamentally two-party.

CLOSING - ~30 sec

So to sum up: Gordon and Katz gave us a clean, simulation-based definition of partial fairness, $1/p$-security, and showed it's achievable exactly when at least one domain or range is polynomial-size. Their gradual release protocol hits the $1/p$ bound, and the impossibility results for equality and Swap show that boundary is tight. This basically settles the open problem Goldreich raised about defining partial fairness in the real/ideal paradigm.
